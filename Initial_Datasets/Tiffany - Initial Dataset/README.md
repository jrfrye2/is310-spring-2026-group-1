<h4>Initial Dataset Documentation</h4>
I built a dataset of 75 professional volleyball players from around the world. For each player, I manually collected their Instagram and Twitter follower counts and how many posts they had made. Since our main theme is on social media's potential effect on players' performance, I plan to expand the data by not just adding more observations, but also by adding more variables, such as average points in the current season, to see if there appears to be trends or patterns.
<h4>How I Collected the Data</h4>
I did not copy this from an existing list as there wasn't much databases I could really find. I started from scratch by first making a list of 75 players by looking at national teams. I tried to get a good mix of men and women, different positions on the court, and players from different continents. Then I had to search for each player on Instagram and Twitter and make sure I found their real account, not a fan page. I looked for verification checkmarks and checked if their official team followed them. Once I was sure, I typed their follower numbers and post counts into a spreadsheet to be later exported as a csv.
<h4>The Tools I Used</h4>
I kept everything organized in a simple spreadsheet which wasn't too difficult. I used Mac's built in numbers application. The hard part was that I could not use any automation. I considered using software or websites (if there are any) to get the numbers for me, but I realized that software can't tell the difference between the real player and a fake account. So I did every single entry by hand, one player at a time. 
<h4>The Decisions I Had to Make</h4>
I honestly didn't make much rules. I tried to make sure that players of different gender and country were represented to eliminate the chance of bias. I also only counted main feed posts, not temporary stories, because I felt like that made more sense. These choices are really simple, but I feel like they allowed the data collected to hopefully be more neutral.
<h4>Challenges I Faced</h4>
I had two challenges. One was that it was and will be difficult to manually record all the social media statistics. The second is that the statistics are constantly changing and these players are constantly getting new followers or making new posts. The numbers I wrote down are only correct for technically the current period of time. My dataset isn't really a live updated dataset.
<h4>What I Noticed</h4>
I feel like even though I haven't added the data for performance yet, it's really obvious which of these players are more popular. Players from countries where volleyball is smaller had way fewer followers, even if they were on similar levels, while players from Japan or Brazil were super popular. This raises questions about how their home country may affect their followers. I also noticed that some players post constantly while others barely post at all, but posting more did not always mean more followers.
<h4>What the Data Can and Cannot Tell Us</h4>
The numbers in this dataset are useful, but I honestly feel like they are pretty limited. They tell us who has the most followers and who posts the most, but there isn't much correlation between posts and follows, raising the question of which I should use to represent their social media activity. I feel like this dataset gives good implications on if popularity (number of followers) corresponds to performance. Like will a player be more popular if they play really well.
<h4>Final Thoughts</h4>
I personally think that even though this dataset is super small, running through them raised a lot of questions in my mind (that will hopefully be solved). I can also see a lot of potential in developing this data to find trends in social media and an athlete's performance.
