# Instructor Feedback on Initial Dataset Submission

Overall, this is a solid first effort and I can tell you put real time and care into building this dataset manually. The sample of 75 players is well within the target range, and I genuinely appreciate the geographic and gender diversity you aimed for, and having players from countries like Japan, Brazil, Turkey, and the Philippines alongside American players gives this dataset real comparative potential. The follower counts and post counts are interesting metrics to start with, though as I'll get into below, they need to be connected to a deeper cultural question before they can really do analytical work. Your `README.md` is also honest and reflective in ways that are genuinely valuable: you acknowledge the snapshot problem, you notice the country-popularity confound on your own, and you flag the tension between post count and follower count as worth pursuing. That kind of critical awareness of your own data is good.

That said, there are a number of things I want to push back on or flag as gaps, both in terms of the assignment requirements and in terms of earlier feedback that hasn't quite been addressed yet.

**1. The Cultural Phenomenon Needs More Conceptual Grounding**

You write that you plan to expand the data by adding variables like average points per season "to see if there appears to be trends or patterns," but the connection between performance metrics and social media metrics needs more conceptual work before it becomes meaningful. I raised this concern in my earlier feedback––that the most obvious hypothesis, that better players have more followers, is somewhat tautological–– and I don't think it's been fully resolved here. The relationship between popularity and performance could run in multiple directions: popular players might post more because they have larger audiences and brand obligations; players who invest heavily in social media might have less time for training; or there might be no meaningful relationship at all. You need to clarify which of these directions you're actually interested in, and why.

What I'd encourage you to pull on instead is the observation you make near the end of the `README.md`––that "posting more did not always mean more followers"––because that's genuinely the most interesting thing in the submission. But you need to push past naming it as a limitation and start asking what it means. Popularity is a complex phenomenon that isn't just about numbers; it's also about the content of posts, the nature of interactions with followers, and the cultural context in which volleyball operates very differently across countries. What would it mean to capture that complexity? You might find, for instance, that you're more interested in players' Instagram profiles as objects of cultural study in their own right (how they construct their personal brand, how they interact with fans) rather than trying to correlate follower counts with on-court performance. There are many interesting angles here, but right now the dataset has metrics without a clear framework connecting them to your research interests. The next phase is about building that framework.

**2. Improvements to the Dataset Construction**

The `README.md` describes how you tried to get "a good mix of men and women, different positions on the court, and players from different continents," but "good mix" is vague as a methodological description. I'm also curious why gender matters for your research question:is it a variable you plan to analyze, or just a sampling concern? And when you say geographic diversity, are you capturing where players were born or where they play professionally? These aren't the same thing, and volleyball players move between leagues frequently. You need to be more specific about how you're defining these categories and why they matter for what you're trying to find out.

On a related note, you write that representing players of different gender and country was meant to "eliminate the chance of bias," and I'd like to push back slightly on that framing. Representing multiple countries doesn't eliminate national or cultural bias, it just changes its shape. And gender categories in sport are more contested than a simple binary suggests. The goal isn't to eliminate these complexities but to be honest about them, which I think you're capable of doing given how reflective the rest of the `README.md` is.

The choice to verify real accounts against fan pages is exactly the kind of methodological rigor this assignment asks for, but it raises a question: why didn't you include links to the social media accounts in the dataset itself? That would make the data much easier for others to verify, and it would open up the possibility of content analysis in the next phase. You might even consider if the profiles themselves might be a more interesting object of study than the aggregate counts. URLs to player profiles, and ideally to specific posts, would be a meaningful addition.

There are also a couple of structural issues in the CSV worth cleaning up. The follower counts are stored as mixed strings ("4.0M," "700K," "13M") rather than consistent numeric values, which will create real problems for any computational analysis; a program won't be able to treat "1.0M" and "1000000" as the same number. There are also two unnamed empty columns trailing every row that should be removed. And the large number of null Twitter values is worth reflecting on analytically rather than just leaving as a gap: is Twitter less relevant for international volleyball players, or were those accounts harder to verify? That could be an interesting finding in itself. Finally, you note that the statistics are constantly changing, which is a real limitation. But rather than just acknowledging it, why not record the date on which you captured each entry? That would at least let future readers contextualize the numbers as a snapshot in time.

**3. The Computational Requirement and Next Steps**

The assignment explicitly requires you to use computational tools to assist your process, and the `README.md` mentions that you considered automation but decided against it entirely because "software can't tell the difference between the real player and a fake account." I understand that reasoning for account verification, but it doesn't mean computation has to play no role at all. Even in a bespoke manual dataset, computation can contribute in other ways. You might consider writing a script to validate or clean your entries, running a basic analysis to surface patterns, or using an LLM to help you pressure-test your categorization decisions. This needs to be addressed going forward, and your final essay will need to explain how computation assisted your process.

Your submission is also missing the third required component: a next steps plan for how you will scale or expand your work computationally after Spring Break. You need to be thinking now about whether you'll pursue Option 1 (scaling up to 500–1,000+ items using automation) or Option 2 (auditing, merging, and analyzing alongside an existing dataset), what methods you'll use, and what you anticipate will change or break when you move from 75 carefully crafted items to something much larger.

Overall, the foundation here is real and I think you are making good progress. The data was collected carefully, and the `README.md` shows you genuinely engaged with the material rather than going through the motions. But the missing components and unresolved conceptual questions are significant, and they're going to need to be priorities as you move into the next phase. Also as a reminder you will need to situate your dataset in peer-reviewed scholarship for the final data essay, so you might start looking at existing literature and that might help you resolve some of these larger questions.
